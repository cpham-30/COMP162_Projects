The dataset I will be using for this analysis project is the Global Road Accidents Dataset by Ankush Panday , which will be imported into R Markdown under the name accidents.

My main motivation for studying this dataset comes from my experience as a driver and an observer who has seen the results of vehicular accidents on the highway during the 1.5 years I have been driving to and from university. As a result of observing near accidents, results of accidents, being stuck in traffic as a consequence of these accidents, I have grown an interest in how vehicular accidents occur. This led me to choose this dataset for my data analysis project.

The dataset I have chosen includes data about accidents from various countries around the world, which consists of 132,000 rows of data and 30 variables as listed:

  • Country - country origin of accident
  • Year - year of accident
  • Month - month of accident
  • Day of Week - day of accident
  • Time of Day - time of accident (i.e.morning, afternoon, evening, or night)
  • Urban/Rural - type of area (i.e.urban or rural)
  • Road Type - type of road (i.e.highway, main road, etc.)
  • Weather Conditions - type of weather (i.e.rainy, snowy, etc.)
  • Visibility Level - distance driver can see (in meters)
  • Number of Vehicles Involved - total number of vehicles in accident
  • Speed Limit - speed limit of the road
  • Driver Age Group - age group of driver involved
  • Driver Gender - gender of driver involved
  • Driver Alcohol Level - alcohol level of driver
  • Driver Fatigue - states if driver is fatigued (1) or not(0)
  • Vehicle Condition - states condition of vehicle (i.e.poor, moderate, good)
  • Pedestrians Involved - total number of pedestrians involved
  • Cyclists Involved - total number of cyclists involved
  • Accident Severity - level of severity (i.e.minor, moderate, severe)
  • Number of Injuries - total number of injuries
  • Number of Fatalities - total number of deaths
  • Emergency Response Time - time emergency response took (in minutes)
  • Traffic Volume - volume of traffic (number of vehicles) on road
  • Road Condition - condition of road as result of weather (i.e.dry, wet, etc.)
  • Accident Cause - cause of accident (i.e.speeding, weather, etc.)
  • Insurance Claims - total number of insurance claims
  • Medical Cost - total cost of medical care for injuries/fatalities
  • Economic Loss - loss of money to fix/compensate accident
  • Region - continental origin of driver
  • Population Density - total number of people located in area of accident

Data Visualizations

ggplot(accidents, aes(`Insurance Claims`,`Number of Fatalities`,
                      color = `Time of Day`)) +
  geom_point()

After going through the quantitative variables in the dataset, I am unable to plot a visible relationship through preliminary data analysis techniques without data manipulation, so I decided to use the scatter plot above. The scatter plot above compares the Number of Fatalities to Insurance Claims, which are colored by the Time of Day that the accidents occurred during.

Based on the scatter plot above, accidents that occur during the afternoon and claimed through insurance are very few, but if there are accidents during this time, they involve 3 to 4 fatalities. Accidents during the morning and claimed range between 0 to 4 fatalities and are the most likely to occur. This may be a result of morning grogginess-related actions. Accidents that occur during the night and evening and claimed have a range of fatalities between 0 to 4 each, with night accidents more likely due to low visibility.

ggplot(accidents, aes(`Road Type`,`Number of Vehicles Involved`)) +
  geom_boxplot()

In the plot above, I created a box plot comparing the Number of Vehicles Involved in accidents to the type of road the accidents occur on. Overall, the least likely type of road for an accident to occur on is the main road. The low number of vehicles involved on main roads may be due to a wider range of road maneuverability and road visibility compared to streets. The more moderate speed limit, compared to highways, may have also aided in reducing the number of vehicles in accidents.

Highways having a larger number of vehicles involved in accidents is expected since highways have higher speed limits, which delays reaction times as a result of drivers not being able to see far ahead if speeding. The large number of vehicles involved may be due to vehicle pileups in accidents or other disastrous actions as a result of speeding, reckless driving, or other causes.

I am surprised that streets would have an equally high number of vehicles involved in accidents to highways. The large number of vehicles involved may have resulted from blind spots along the streets that led to delays in reaction times of the driver. Since streets are usually narrower compared to highways and main roads, this may have factored into how much room drivers can maneuver there vehicles to avoid accidents, which may have led to a higher number of vehicles involved in accidents on this type of road.

From this plot, this leads me to the question: How does the vehicles condition affect the number of vehicles involved in accidents?

ggplot(accidents, aes(`Road Type`,`Number of Vehicles Involved`,
                      fill = `Vehicle Condition`)) +
  geom_boxplot()

So, I created the box plot above to identify any differences between Vehicle Condition, Number of Vehicles Involved, and Road Type. From the plot above, vehicles in good condition are least likely to be involved in accidents while vehicles in poor condition are most likely to be involved.

On average, vehicles in good condition are most likely to be involved in accidents on streets. On the other hand, vehicles of moderate condition are most likely to be involved in accidents on streets and highways while vehicles in poor condition are most likely to be involved in accidents on highways.

This also leads me to another question: How does the number of vehicles involved and their conditions differ between urban and rural areas?

ggplot(accidents, aes(`Urban/Rural`,`Number of Vehicles Involved`,
                      fill = `Vehicle Condition`)) +
  geom_boxplot()

On average, the vehicles of moderate condition are most likely to be involved in accidents in both urban and rural areas. Vehicles in good condition are most likely to be involved in rural areas while those in poor condition are most likely to be involved in urban areas.

The difference in poor vehicles may be due to the driving distances and the terrain in the different areas. Since the terrain in rural areas are harsher on vehicles compared to urban areas, drivers are more likely to keep good or moderate condition of their vehicles so that they may be able to drive through rural areas more easily. And as a result of a nicer driving environment that urban areas are more likely able to provide, the need to upkeep vehicle conditions is low.

Conclusion

In conclusion, there is little correlation between the quantitative variables from the original dataset, which leads to negligible relationships between them. However, there is a significant relationship between a select few categorical variables and some quantitative variables that give insight on where accidents are likely to occur and their severity.

This leads to my conclusion that main roads are safer compared to highways and streets, most likely as a result of moderate speed limits and easy maneuverability and road visibility. Both urban and rural areas have similar rates of accidents, but the conditions of the drivers vehicle is a major determinant in how many vehicles are involved. From this, I can also conclude that to lower the probability of getting into accidents, it is vital to maintain moderate or better vehicle conditions. If an accident occurs, a minor one leads to more economic loss than more moderate or severe accidents as more money is used to repair and clean a small-scaled accident that could be put to better use in more severe cases.

Overall, accidents can occur anywhere, with most occurring along highways and streets. The condition of the drivers vehicle is very important as it can determine how many other vehicles collide alongside the original vehicle that caused the accident to occur. Finally, no matter how severe the accident is, there would be an economic loss in cleaning up the scene, fixing vehicles, and paying medical costs and insurance claims, but the loss from minor accidents, which is greater than the loss from moderate and severe accidents, could be put to better use in other areas.
